Inter-Theoretic Explanation and Unity of Science

Theory Summary and Key Insights

Explanation, Prediction, and the D-N Model

The Deductive-Nomological model, or D-N model, represents a scientific explanation or prediction as a deductive argument. A general law and an initial condition logically entail a phenomenon. If the phenomenon has already been observed, the argument is treated as an explanation; if it has not yet been observed, it is treated as a prediction.

A successful definition must apply only to the things being defined and to all of them. A definition is too weak when it includes cases that are not genuine instances, and too strong when it excludes genuine instances. Critics argue that the D-N model has both defects.

  • Symmetry problem: Newtonian laws and Monday's stellar configuration can permit calculation of Sunday's configuration, but Monday does not explain Sunday. Similarly, a flagpole's shadow can be used to calculate its height without explaining that height.
  • Excluded causal explanations: Untreated syphilis can explain paresis even though paresis does not deductively follow from untreated syphilis. A spilled cup can also explain a carpet stain without an explicitly stated universal law.
Key Insight: Logical derivability and causal explanation can point in different directions. Being able to calculate or infer a phenomenon from some information does not establish that the information caused or explained the phenomenon.

Prediction concerns logical direction: a phenomenon is predicted when it follows logically from the assumptions. Explanation concerns causal direction: the proposed explanation must be a cause of the phenomenon. When logical and causal directions coincide, one argument may serve both roles. When they diverge, a prediction need not be an explanation, and an explanation need not be a prediction. The D-N model is therefore a model of prediction but not a general model of explanation.

The alternative causal-power model says that one event explains another when it causes it. Its central problem is that causality itself has not been defined, so it attempts to clarify explanation by appealing to something even less understood.

Inter-Theoretic Explanation

Inter-theoretic explanation asks when one theory explains another. The proposed criterion is that theory T1 explains theory T2 when T2 logically follows from T1. This relation can be considered synchronically, between theories operating at different levels at the same time, or diachronically, between theories across scientific history.

In the synchronic ideal, a theory is explained by a more basic theory involving smaller underlying objects. If chemistry follows logically from physics, physics explains chemistry. The thermodynamics and statistical-mechanics example illustrates the intended structure: heating is identified with increased molecular motion, while increased pressure is identified with more collisions against container walls. The lower-level theory can then derive the higher-level relationship.

This approach requires two stages:

  1. Bridge statements: establish identities between terms in the theories.
  2. Derivation: derive the laws of the theory being explained from the laws of the explaining theory.

Such reductions are meant to provide additional validity for both theories and a unified picture of science. The resulting ideal is called the unity of science: all knowledge would form one organized structure. Its methodological counterpart is methodological individualism, according to which events at the level of wholes occur because of events at the level of individuals, rather than the reverse.

Key Insight: Inter-theoretic derivation requires more than parallel regularities. Bridge statements must connect the vocabularies of the two theories before the higher-level law can be derived from the lower-level law.

Teleology and the Biological Challenge

A causal explanation explains a later event by an earlier event, as when rain explains subsequent grass growth. A teleological explanation is goal-directed and explains an earlier event by a later goal, as when rain is said to occur in order for grass to grow. Physics and chemistry came to be regarded as causal sciences, whereas biology, psychology, and sociology appeared predominantly teleological.

This difference initially threatened the unity of science because a goal-directed science seemed impossible to derive from causal sciences. Biological functions appeared to explain biological traits: a sunflower turns toward the sun in order to grow, or a giraffe has a long neck in order to reach high leaves. Adaptation also supported arguments for intelligent design.

Darwinian evolution replaced apparent purpose with a causal history. In the giraffe example, random variation produced a slightly longer neck. Access to more food increased survival and reproduction, so the trait spread over generations. The neck did not arise for the purpose of reaching leaves; it persisted because it had previously increased survival.

The general mechanism combines heritability, variation, and differential fitness. These conditions produce non-random reproduction, or natural selection, and thereby increasing adaptation. Any system with these three features can appear purposeful without a guiding hand.

Key Insight: Natural selection does not deny that traits have functions. It changes the explanatory direction: a useful effect does not act as a future goal that creates the trait; past variation, differential survival, and reproduction explain why the trait became common.

Biology as a Historical Science

Darwin moved biology to the causal side of the explanatory divide, but a new obstacle emerged. Every adaptation can create selective pressure on other species, producing a coevolutionary arms race known as the Red Queen dynamic. Biology consequently depends on continuing historical change.

On an extreme view, this historical variability prevents universal biological laws, with natural selection proposed as the only true biological law. Biological explanations often use tendencies, models, and broad generalizations that admit exceptions, unlike the stable and exceptionless laws attributed to physics and chemistry. Some critics argue that even natural selection is not a genuine law because it depends on background conditions and can fail when those conditions are disrupted.

This qualitative difference raises doubts about complete reduction of biology to chemistry and physics. Because psychology and sociology rely on biology, it also raises doubts about exceptionless laws in those higher-level sciences.

Psychology, Brain Science, and Reduction

The reductionist ideal for psychology models mental-to-brain reduction on the reduction of thermodynamics to statistical mechanics. Bridge statements would identify mental states with brain states, such as pain with brain state A and the desire to eliminate pain with brain state B. Brain laws would then derive psychological laws.

Several objections challenge this project:

  • Multiple realization: Fodor argues that one mental state can be realized by more than one physical state, as illustrated by possible human, octopus, or Martian pain. A single psychological law may therefore correspond to many physical laws, blocking one-to-one translation.
  • Accessibility: brain states appear publicly accessible, while mental states are accessible only to the subject. The toaster response distinguishes access from performance: everyone may observe an action although only the toaster performs it. Similarly, a physical mental activity could be publicly observable while being performed by one subject.
  • Chinese Room: Searle argues that correct input-output processing is insufficient for understanding or consciousness. The systems response claims that the whole room understands even if the person does not. Searle replies by imagining that the person memorizes the entire rulebook while still lacking understanding.
  • Black-and-White Mary: Jackson imagines Mary knowing every physical fact about color vision but learning something when she first sees red. The intended conclusion is that experiential facts extend beyond physical facts.
  • Explanatory gap: a complete physical account appears not to explain why experience exists at all or why a particular physical state is accompanied by this experience rather than another. A physically identical being without experience, called a zombie, is presented as conceivable.
  • Metaphysical or windmill argument: observation inside a brain reveals physical processes rather than experiential characteristics, suggesting that material mechanisms cannot explain perception.

A response to the metaphysical argument distinguishes an experience from an experience of that experience. If Jack's experience is his brain state, Alice's observation of that state gives Alice an experience of Jack's experience. Jack's experience of a bus and Alice's experience of Jack's experience of a bus are different objects of experience, so their qualitative difference does not refute the identity of mental and brain states.

Key Insight: The objections to psychological reduction target different steps. Multiple realization challenges bridge identities, the Chinese Room challenges the sufficiency of input-output processing, and Mary and the explanatory gap challenge whether physical facts capture experience itself.
