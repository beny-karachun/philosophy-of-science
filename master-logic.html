How to read the arrows

→A conditional inside a statement: “if P, then Q.” It can be true or false.
⟹An inference: the statement on the right follows from the premises on the left.
⟺An equivalence: the rule can be used in either direction.
MPModus ponensP → Q, P ⟹ Q

If a conditional is true and its antecedent P holds, you may conclude its consequent Q.

ExampleIf it rains, the street gets wet. It rains. Therefore, the street gets wet.
MTModus tollensP → Q, ¬Q ⟹ ¬P

If P would guarantee Q, but Q is false, then P must also be false.

ExampleIf the sample is copper, it conducts electricity. It does not conduct. Therefore, it is not copper.
HSHypothetical syllogismP → Q, Q → R ⟹ P → R

Chain two conditionals when the result of the first is the condition of the second.

ExampleIf the theory is confirmed, confidence rises. If confidence rises, funding continues. Therefore, if the theory is confirmed, funding continues.
TransContrapositionP → Q ⟺ ¬Q → ¬P

A conditional is logically equivalent to reversing and negating both of its parts.

Example“If it is a whale, it is a mammal” is equivalent to “If it is not a mammal, it is not a whale.”
ImplImplicationP → Q ⟺ ¬P ∨ Q

A conditional says that either its antecedent is false or its consequent is true. The two forms are equivalent.

Example“If the trial ran, data were recorded” is equivalent to “The trial did not run, or data were recorded.”
DSDisjunctive syllogismP ∨ Q, ¬P ⟹ Q

If at least one of two alternatives is true and one is ruled out, the other must be true.

ExampleThe error is in the sensor or the software. It is not in the sensor. Therefore, it is in the software.
AddAdditionP ⟹ P ∨ Q

From a true statement P, you may infer a disjunction containing P, because one true alternative makes the disjunction true.

ExampleThe result was measured. Therefore, the result was measured or simulated.
SimpSimplificationP ∧ Q ⟹ P

When a conjunction is true, each of its parts is true and may be taken separately.

ExampleThe study was peer-reviewed and replicated. Therefore, the study was peer-reviewed.

Pattern guide

Compare the direction of each argument: from a group to a case, from cases to a group, or between similar cases.

Statistical syllogismMost F are G; x is F; therefore, x is probably G.
Inductive generalizationMost examined F are G in a representative sample; therefore, most F are G.
Combined generalizationA generalization from a sample is applied to a particular case.
Analogical inferencex and y are similar; a relevant truth about x is projected onto y.
