Logic, Scientific Inquiry, and Falsifiability

Theory Summary and Key Insights

Deductive and Non-Deductive Arguments

A deductive argument is valid when its premises necessitate its conclusion. If the premises are true, the conclusion cannot be false. A non-deductive argument is strong when the premises make the conclusion more likely to be true than false, expressed as $P(c) > 0.5$.

This distinction affects how arguments respond to additional information. Adding premises cannot turn a valid deductive argument into an invalid one, whereas new premises may strengthen or weaken a non-deductive argument.

  • Modus ponens: From if $P$ then $Q$, together with $P$, infer $Q$.
  • Modus tollens: From if $P$ then $Q$, together with not-$Q$, infer not-$P$.
  • Hypothetical syllogism: From if $P$ then $Q$ and if $Q$ then $R$, infer if $P$ then $R$.
  • Transposition: If $P$ then $Q$ is equivalent to if not-$Q$ then not-$P$.
  • Disjunctive syllogism: From $P$ or $Q$, together with not-$P$, infer $Q$.

Natural deduction proves validity by deriving a conclusion from premises through recognized rules of inference. A familiar error is to infer $P$ from if $P$ then $Q$ and $Q$. That form does not match modus ponens or modus tollens.

Induction and Its Main Forms

Inductive arguments support conclusions without making them certain. A statistical syllogism applies a general statistical claim to an individual: most or all $F$ objects are $G$, an object $x$ is $F$, and therefore $x$ is probably $G$.

An inductive generalization moves from examined cases to a larger class. It requires that a representative amount has been examined. A combined inductive generalization first generalizes from examined cases and then applies the resulting generalization to an individual. An analogical inference transfers a property from one object to another on the basis of extensive similarity.

Induction has an important limitation in theory construction. Its conclusion cannot introduce terms absent from its observational premises. It can generalize that all examined $F$ objects were $G$ into a claim that all $F$ objects are $G$, but it cannot thereby invent a new theoretical concept such as mass, gravitational field, spin, super-ego, or demand.

Abduction and Theory Under-Determination

Abduction is inference to the best explanation. Its general form is: phenomenon $Q$ occurs; hypothesis $P$ is the best explanation of $Q$; therefore, $P$. The hypothesis must entail the phenomenon, but the observations do not deductively guarantee that the selected explanation is true.

There is no mechanical method for inventing the explanatory hypothesis. Inventiveness, intuition, and luck are involved. Moreover, another explanation $P^*$ may entail the same phenomenon. This is theory under-determination: the same observations can support multiple explanatory theories.

A crucial experiment addresses under-determination by locating a point at which competing theories make contradictory predictions and then testing those predictions through observation or experiment.

Key Insight: Abduction can introduce new theoretical terms because it proposes an explanation rather than merely extending the vocabulary of observed cases. Its creative power is also its logical limitation: several incompatible hypotheses may explain the same evidence.

The Positivist Scientific Method

The positivist scientific method treats observation and logic as the legitimate sources of knowledge. It distinguishes the relationship between theories and observations from the relationship among theories.

  1. Formulation or context of discovery: Begin with observations requiring explanation and formulate a theory from which those observations logically follow.
  2. Testing or context of justification: derive additional observational implications and determine whether they occur.
  3. Application: use the theory to predict observations not yet made or explain observations already made.

The relationship among theories may be synchronic or diachronic. Synchronically, a more basic theory explains a less basic theory in another field at the same time. Diachronically, a newer theory in a field explains the roughly correct theory that preceded it. In both cases, explanation requires the explained theory to be logically derived from the explaining theory.

Matching Logical Tools to Scientific Tasks

The central scientific tasks are formulation, refutation, verification, and confirmation. Their logical structures differ:

  • Formulation: Deduction cannot derive a general hypothesis from particular observations. Induction can formulate only hypotheses that generalize observational statements. Abduction is the main route to hypotheses containing new theoretical terms.
  • Refutation: A hypothesis can be deductively refuted by modus tollens: if $H$ then $e$; not-$e$; therefore, not-$H$.
  • Verification: General hypotheses cannot be verified from particular observations. Verification would require certainty, so only deduction could provide it, but deduction cannot move from particular observational claims to a more informative general claim.
  • Confirmation: Confirmation is inductive and therefore non-certain. The observed occurrence of predicted consequences raises the plausibility of a hypothesis but does not verify it.

The apparent confirmation form is: if $H$ then $e$; $e$ occurs; therefore, probably $H$. For a general hypothesis that all $F$ objects are $G$, the evidence is that all examined $F$ objects are $G$. The move from examined cases to all cases is inductive generalization. Abduction is not the confirmation method in this framework because abduction is used while searching for a hypothesis, whereas confirmation begins with a hypothesis already given.

Key Insight: The asymmetry between refutation and verification follows from logic. A failed predicted consequence can support the certain deductive conclusion not-$H$, but any finite collection of particular successes still falls short of deductively establishing a general hypothesis.
Key Insight: Formulation and confirmation can both involve induction, but they are not the same task. In formulation, induction generalizes observed cases into a limited kind of hypothesis; in confirmation, it evaluates an already available hypothesis through its successfully observed implications.

Popper, Falsification, and Demarcation

Karl Popper sought a demarcation principle distinguishing scientific theories from non-scientific ones. He was concerned that Marxism, Freudian psychoanalysis, and Adlerian psychology appeared able to explain every possible event, including an event and its opposite, while still treating each outcome as confirmation.

Popper rejected induction because it does not provide certainty and therefore rejected confirmation as scientifically authoritative. Science should instead formulate hypotheses and actively attempt to refute them deductively. Scientists should derive risky predictions whose failure would count against the theory.

A theory is falsifiable when it specifies observations that could refute it through deductive inference. Popper's demarcation principle states that a theory is scientific if and only if it is falsifiable. The more possible observations that could refute a theory, the more scientific it is considered within this account.

Theories that survive many serious refutation attempts differ from untested conjectures. In the presented Popperian account, surviving repeated tests raises a theory's plausibility without turning the surviving results into verification.

The Eddington Experiment

Einstein's General Theory of Relativity predicted that light would bend around massive objects because of spacetime curvature. During the 1919 solar eclipse expedition, Eddington observed the apparent displacement of stars near the Sun, supporting the predicted bending of starlight.

The case illustrates falsifiability because failure to observe the predicted light bending could have refuted the theory. Its scientific significance does not arise merely from obtaining a favorable observation, but from exposing the theory to an observational result that could have counted against it.

Key Insight: A theory that accommodates both an observation and its negation without identifying any adverse outcome is not strengthened by its flexibility in Popper's framework. That flexibility removes the possibility of a genuine deductive refutation and therefore undermines its scientific status.
