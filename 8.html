Laws of Nature and Scientific Prediction

Theory Summary and Key Insights

The Logical Tasks of the Scientific Method

The scientific method can be represented as a set of instructions governing several different tasks. Theory formulation uses abduction: an observation is identified, a hypothesis is judged to be its best explanation, and the hypothesis is adopted. Confirmation uses induction: if a hypothesis predicts an observation and the observation occurs, the hypothesis is regarded as probably supported. Refutation uses deduction, specifically modus tollens: if a hypothesis implies an observation but the observation does not occur, the hypothesis is rejected.

  • Formulation: observation; the best explanation is $H$; therefore, $H$.
  • Confirmation: if $H$, then $e$; $e$; therefore, probably $H$.
  • Refutation: if $H$, then $e$; not $e$; therefore, not $H$.
  • Prediction: a law of nature plus initial conditions deductively yields an expected observation.

Problems of Induction and Refutation

Inductive formulation and confirmation face problems such as the Ravens Paradox and the new riddle of induction. The same observation can support strange predictions when it is redescribed in a logically permitted but scientifically inappropriate way. Consequently, a simple inductive instruction manual can produce absurd results, yet no satisfactory replacement procedure has been established.

The problem with refutation is different. Modus tollens itself is valid, but a hypothesis is never tested alone. It is tested together with background assumptions. If $H^*$ represents those assumptions, the actual structure is:

$$H + H^* \rightarrow e,\quad \neg e,\quad \therefore \neg(H + H^*)$$

This entails that either $H$ or some part of $H^*$ is false; it does not identify which one. The Uranus anomaly was handled by changing background assumptions through the introduction of Neptune, whereas the Mercury anomaly ultimately led to replacing Newtonian theory with relativity.

Key Insight: Induction and refutation fail in different places. Induction has a problem with the inferential procedure itself, while deductive refutation uses valid logic but cannot ordinarily be applied to an isolated hypothesis.

Popper and Falsifiability

Popper proposed falsifiability as a criterion of demarcation: a theory is scientific if observations could refute it by deductive reasoning. He contrasted Einsteinian relativity with theories he regarded as pseudoscientific because they could accommodate confirmation but could not, in principle, be refuted. The Duhem–Quine problem threatens this criterion: if observations test only a theory-plus-background-assumptions package, no individual theory can be conclusively falsified. Taken strictly, Popper's criterion would then classify every theory as unscientific.

Prediction and the Need for Laws of Nature

A scientific prediction combines a law of nature with initial conditions. For example, the claim that any body of water expands when it freezes, together with the condition that a lake will freeze, yields the prediction that the lake's volume will increase. A merely true generalization is insufficient. A perfect past association between Lucy's attendance and her team's victories may be accidental and therefore does not ground a scientific prediction about the next game.

This creates a definitional problem. The proposal that a law is simply a true generalization is too weak because it includes accidental regularities. Adding necessity appears promising: a law would be a true generalization that is also necessary. The central dispute is whether such necessity exists and can be understood.

Three Arguments Against Necessity

  1. Epistemic argument: epistemic empiricism permits justified belief only in what is empirically experienced. Experience reveals constant conjunctions, not necessary connections, so belief in worldly necessity appears unjustified.
  2. Metaphysical argument: Hume's fork divides statements into necessary and a priori, or non-necessary and a posteriori. Scientific laws are known a posteriori, so the argument classifies them as non-necessary.
  3. Semantic argument: semantic empiricism equates meaning with empirical conditions of verification. Because necessity is not empirically experienced, statements invoking it are declared meaningless.

The epistemic argument concerns justification, the metaphysical argument concerns what kinds of truths exist, and the semantic argument concerns meaning. Skepticism here targets necessary connections in the world, not mathematical or definitional necessities such as $2 + 3 = 5$.

Key Insight: Constant conjunction and necessary connection are not the same claim. Repeatedly observing one type of event after another establishes a pattern, but the anti-Humean must still explain why that pattern reflects a fixed connection rather than coincidence.

Humean Approaches

Humean views reject necessity and seek another criterion for distinguishing laws from accidents. Their motivation includes the thought that a world containing exactly the same sequence of observable events but no necessary connections would be empirically indistinguishable from ours. In a sufficiently large collection of unrelated events, flexible categorization may reveal apparent order, so supposed laws might project human expectations onto phenomena.

Several simple Humean criteria fail:

  • Requiring a generalization not to mention specific people or places is too weak, because the accidental claim that all pieces of gold weigh less than one ton satisfies it.
  • The same requirement is also too strong, because genuine laws can have restricted application, such as the free-fall relation $x = 0.5gt^2$ on Earth's surface.
  • Requiring exclusively general terms is too weak because singular descriptions can be paraphrased into general-looking descriptions without removing their accidental character.

The Ramsey–Lewis view is identified as the strongest Humean approach, but its details are outside the material considered here.

Anti-Humean Replies

Anti-Humeans accept necessity and define a law as a true generalization that is also necessary. Referential theory challenges Hume's fork by separating how a truth is known from whether it is true in every possible world. Proper names are rigid designators: each designates the same object in every possible world. Thus an identity between two names of the same object can be discovered a posteriori while being necessary. The identity between Madonna and Louise Ciccone is used to illustrate this category. Conversely, a statement can be contingent yet a priori.

The anti-Humean reply to epistemic empiricism is an argument by absurd consequence. Science accepts electrons, forces, fields, and molecular structures despite experiencing only their effects or traces. If belief were justified only by direct empirical experience, much of science would have to be abandoned. Constant conjunction might similarly provide indirect reason to infer necessity, although this reply risks also licensing a necessary connection in accidental cases such as Lucy's attendance and victory.

The reply to semantic empiricism is parallel. If terms lacking direct empirical verification were meaningless, claims about forces and fields would also become meaningless. Because that cost undermines central scientific theories, anti-Humeans reject the semantic premise.

Key Insight: Refuting Hume's fork only shows that necessary a posteriori truths are possible. It removes one argument against necessary laws, but it does not by itself identify which observed regularities are laws.

The Armstrong–Dartski–Tuli View and the Remaining Problem

The constructive anti-Humean view associated with Armstrong, Dartski, and Tuli holds that objects have properties and that fixed relationships exist between properties. A lake expands when frozen because of a fixed relationship between being water and increasing in volume when frozen. By contrast, even if Galileo's wearing white were always followed by cloudiness, no corresponding connection between those properties would make the pattern a law.

This account explains laws through nomological necessity, the necessity belonging to laws of nature. Its central criticism is that the nature of this connection still requires explanation. Calling it nomological necessity risks naming precisely what was supposed to be explained.

Key Insight: The dispute affects the scientific method of prediction itself. Without an accepted criterion separating laws from accidental generalizations, there is no settled procedure for deciding which deductively valid predictions are genuinely scientific.
