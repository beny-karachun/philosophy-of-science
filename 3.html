Logic and Scientific Reasoning

Theory Summary and Key Insights

Scientific Method and Logical Empiricism

The scientific method is a set of guidelines for producing reliable theories and using them to explain and predict natural phenomena. A comprehensive method must address theory formation, confirmation and refutation, explanation and prediction, relations among theories, and the role of values in science.

Logical empiricism, associated with the Vienna Circle, treats empirical observation and logic as the two tools on which scientific practice should rely. Observations and theories are expressed through propositions, while logic connects those propositions. The central question is whether these two resources can successfully perform all the main tasks of science.

  • Formation: Find a theory $T$ from which an observation $E$ can be logically derived.
  • Confirmation: If $T$ entails $E$ and $E$ occurs, the proposed method regards $E$ as confirming $T$.
  • Refutation: If $T$ entails $E$ but $E$ does not occur, the method regards $T$ as refuted.
  • Prediction: $T$ predicts $E$ when $T$ entails $E$ and $E$ has not yet been observed.
  • Explanation: $T$ explains $E$ when $T$ entails an already observed $E$ and would have permitted its prediction beforehand.

A test is especially meaningful when its prediction is risky: the predicted event would be unlikely to occur unless the theory were true. Once a theory passes risky tests, it is treated as reliable enough to generate further predictions.

Key Insight: Prediction and explanation share the same logical relation: the theory entails the observation. Their difference is temporal. A prediction concerns an event not yet observed, whereas an explanation concerns an event already observed that the theory could have predicted beforehand.

Relations among Scientific Theories

Synchronic relations concern theories in different disciplines at the same time. The Unity of Science Ideal arranges disciplines hierarchically so that the axioms of a higher-level science become theorems derivable from a lower-level science.

Diachronic relations concern older and newer theories within a discipline. An old theory may be discarded after refutation, or it may be reduced to a special case of a newer, broader theory. This second pattern supports the Cumulative Science Ideal. Both synchronic unification and diachronic reduction involve one theory explaining another.

Arguments, Validity, and Strength

Logic concerns the substantiation of claims. An argument consists of one or more premises offered in support of a conclusion. Arguments divide into two main kinds:

  • A deductive argument claims that its premises necessitate its conclusion. It is valid when the conclusion follows necessarily and invalid otherwise.
  • A non-deductive argument claims only that its premises support the conclusion or raise its plausibility. It is strong when the premises make the conclusion more likely true than false, represented as $P(c) > 0.5$, and weak otherwise.

Validity is not a matter of degree, whereas strength is. Adding premises cannot change a valid deductive argument into an invalid one, but additional information may change the strength of a non-deductive argument.

A deductive argument is sound when it is valid and its premises are true. A non-deductive argument is reliable when it is strong and its premises are true. Truth and falsity apply to sentences or premises; validity, strength, soundness, and reliability apply to arguments.

Key Insight: A valid argument need not be sound. Validity evaluates whether the conclusion follows from the premises, while soundness additionally requires the premises to be true. Thus, an argument may have flawless deductive form but fail to establish a true conclusion because a premise is false.

Deductive Rules for Conditionals

Natural deduction proves validity by deriving a conclusion through accepted rules of inference. For conditional propositions, the principal rules are:

  • Modus Ponens: From if $P$ then $Q$, and $P$, infer $Q$.
  • Modus Tollens: From if $P$ then $Q$, and not $Q$, infer not $P$.
  • Hypothetical Syllogism: From if $P$ then $Q$, and if $Q$ then $R$, infer if $P$ then $R$.
  • Transposition: If $P$ then $Q$ is equivalent to if not $Q$ then not $P$.
  • Implementation: If $P$ then $Q$ is equivalent to not $P$ or $Q$.
  • Negation of Conditional: Not if $P$ then $Q$ is equivalent to $P$ and not $Q$.

Transposition, implementation, and negation of conditional are bidirectional conversions. They restate the same logical content, so either form may be converted into the other.

Rules for Conjunctions and Disjunctions

  • Disjunctive Syllogism: From $P$ or $Q$, and not $P$, infer $Q$.
  • Constructive Dilemma: From if $P$ then $R$, if $Q$ then $S$, and $P$ or $Q$, infer $R$ or $S$.
  • Destructive Dilemma: From the same paired conditionals and not $R$ or not $S$, infer not $P$ or not $Q$.
  • Addition: From $P$, infer $P$ or $Q$.
  • Simplification: From $P$ and $Q$, infer $P$.
  • De Morgan conversion: Not ($P$ or $Q$) is equivalent to not $P$ and not $Q$; not ($P$ and $Q$) is equivalent to not $P$ or not $Q$.
Key Insight: Validity proofs often require several individually simple steps. A disjunction may first be produced by Addition, a conditional may then be applied by Modus Ponens, and one part of the resulting conjunction may finally be extracted by Simplification.

Inductive Reasoning

Inductive arguments are non-deductive and commonly move between samples, target groups, and individual cases. Their conclusions are supported rather than necessitated.

  • Inductive generalization: If most examined members of a representative sample of $F$ are $G$, infer that most $F$ are $G$.
  • Statistical syllogism: If most $F$ are $G$ and $x$ is an $F$, infer that $x$ is $G$.
  • Integrated inductive generalization: Combine representative sample evidence with the fact that $x$ belongs to the target group to infer that $x$ has the observed property.
  • Analogical syllogism: If most of what is true of $x$ is also true of $y$, and $x$ has property $F$, infer that $y$ has property $F$.

Representativeness is essential to inductive generalization because it connects the examined sample to the target group. Even a strong inductive conclusion remains less than deductively guaranteed; later premises may strengthen or weaken it.

Key Insight: Inductive generalization and statistical syllogism move in opposite directions. Generalization moves from a representative sample to a population-level claim, while statistical syllogism applies a population-level claim to a particular member.
