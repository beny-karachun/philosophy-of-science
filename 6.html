Induction and Its Problems in Science

Theory Summary and Key Insights

The Positivist Framework of Scientific Inquiry

Logical positivism presents scientific inquiry as a set of tasks performed using two reliable sources of knowledge: observation and logic. Its principal logical tools are deduction, induction, and abduction. The scientific method is divided into theory formulation, theory evaluation, theory application, and relationships between theories.

  • Formulation: Abduction proposes a hypothesis $H$ as the best explanation of observed evidence $e$. Induction can also formulate a theory when the theory uses the same observational terms as the observations.
  • Confirmation: Induction moves from a predicted and observed consequence to probable support for a hypothesis: if $H$, then $e$; $e$; therefore, probably $H$.
  • Refutation: Deduction uses the form if $H$, then $e$; not $e$; therefore, not $H$.
  • Application: Prediction and explanation use deduction from a law or theory together with initial conditions.
Key Insight: The same observation can play very different logical roles. An observed consequence supports a hypothesis only inductively, whereas failure of a consequence that follows from the hypothesis supports refutation deductively.

Explanation, Prediction, and Relations Between Theories

The deductive-nomological model applies the standards of prediction to explanation. A phenomenon $E$ is scientifically explained when it follows deductively from a general law $L$ and initial conditions $I$. The same structure can predict $E$ before it occurs or explain it after it occurs.

Relationships between theories are also represented as explanations by deduction:

  • Synchronous explanation connects theories operating at the same time in different levels or fields. A more basic theory explains a higher-level theory through bridge laws that translate their different terms. For example, identifying heat with molecular kinetic motion connects statistical mechanics with gas theory.
  • Diachronic explanation concerns old and new theories. A newer and better theory should derive the relevant old theory, often under restricted conditions. Einstein's relative-velocity formula yields the Newtonian formula when the relevant velocities are much smaller than the speed of light.

Where Induction Enters Science

Induction is used primarily for confirmation. It generalizes from examined cases to all cases: all examined $F$ objects are $G$; therefore, all $F$ objects are $G$. It may also formulate hypotheses when the conclusion remains in observational language. A hypothesis stated in non-observable terms cannot be produced by simple inductive generalization and instead requires abduction.

Induction does not make its conclusion certain. It provides support by increasing the probability of the conclusion. Consequently, any defect in induction affects confirmation and also those cases of theory formulation that rely on inductive generalization.

Hume's Problem of Induction

Hume's problem concerns the justification of induction itself. A proposed defense might claim that all examined inductions have succeeded and therefore all inductions are reliable. This defense is circular because it uses the very inductive rule whose reliability it is supposed to establish. It assumes that past success can be generalized to unexamined cases, which is precisely the point in need of justification.

Key Insight: Hume's objection is not merely that a particular induction might fail. It challenges the non-circular justification of the general practice of moving from examined cases to unexamined cases.

Hempel's Ravens Paradox

The logical transformation called Trans states that all $F$ are $G$ if and only if all non-$G$ are non-$F$. Thus, all ravens are black is logically equivalent to all non-black things are non-ravens.

This equivalence creates a problem for both formulation and confirmation by induction. Observing a brown table, a green phone, or a white shoe provides instances of non-black things that are not ravens. Generalizing from such observations yields all non-black things are non-ravens and, by Trans, all ravens are black. The same method can generate or confirm claims about other raven colors from apparently irrelevant non-ravens.

Hempel accepts the surprising consequence. The universal claim applies to every object: only a non-black raven violates it, while a black raven, a black bowl, and a white shoe all fit it. On his account, the feeling of paradox results from our psychological tendency to focus only on ravens.

Bayesian Degrees of Confirmation

Bayesianism treats confirmation as a matter of degree. An observation $e$ changes support for a hypothesis $H$ by the ratio $P(e\mid H)/P(e)$. If $e$ follows logically from $H$, then $P(e\mid H)=1$, so the relevant factor is $1/P(e)$.

A black raven is comparatively improbable and therefore significantly increases support for the hypothesis that all ravens are black. A white shoe or another non-black non-raven is highly probable and increases support only slightly. This explains how such an object can technically confirm the hypothesis without providing substantial evidential weight.

Hempel's and the Bayesian responses address the confirmation version of the paradox, but they do not prevent induction from formulating absurd hypotheses.

Goodman's New Riddle of Induction

Goodman's riddle shows that the same objects can be described using different predicates that agree on all present observations but support incompatible future predictions. The predicate bleen applies to an object if it was first observed before 2050 and is black, or if it was not first observed before 2050 and is green.

Before 2050, every observed black raven is both black and bleen. Induction therefore supports both all ravens are black and all ravens are bleen. Yet the first hypothesis predicts that ravens first observed after 2050 will be black, while the second predicts that they will be green.

A proposed ban on time-dependent terms does not settle the issue. A speaker whose basic vocabulary contains bleen and grack can describe black and green as the complex, time-dependent predicates. The distinction between simple and artificial predicates therefore depends on the language used.

Key Insight: Goodman's riddle reveals that observations alone do not determine which description should be projected into the future. Identical evidence can support incompatible generalizations when it is classified under different predicates.

Entrenchment and Its Limitation

Goodman proposes restricting induction to entrenched terms: terms that have been used successfully in many generalizations and continue to be so used. Black is entrenched, whereas bleen is not. Similarly, non-black and non-raven lack the required history of successful generalization, so they cannot be used to obtain confirmation through the Ravens Paradox.

This proposal addresses both paradoxes by excluding problematic predicates. Its major limitation is that it is too restrictive: if only already entrenched terms are admissible, science cannot readily introduce new terms such as electron, mass, magnetic field, or DNA.

Key Insight: The proposed solutions involve a trade-off. Permissive induction admits irrelevant evidence and artificial predicates, while Goodman's restrictive criterion threatens the conceptual innovation on which scientific progress depends.

Overall Conclusion

The positivist method is attractive because it appears simple, clear, and grounded only in observation and logic. The problems of Hume, Hempel, and Goodman show, however, that an instruction manual based on unrestricted induction can justify circular reasoning, treat apparently irrelevant observations as confirmation, and project artificial classifications into the future. No satisfactory revision presented here fully preserves both disciplined confirmation and the freedom to formulate genuinely new scientific concepts.
