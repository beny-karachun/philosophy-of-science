Logic, Explanation, and Theory Change in Science

Theory Summary and Key Insights

Logical Positivism and the Tasks of Science

Logical positivism treats observation and logic as the only reliable sources of scientific knowledge. Its ambition is to provide clear procedures for the major tasks of science: formulating, confirming, and refuting theories; explaining and predicting observations; and explaining relationships between theories.

The scientific method can be divided into four broad stages:

  1. Theory formulation: a theory is formulated from observations in the discovery context.
  2. Theory evaluation: further observations test the theory in the justification context, producing confirmation or refutation.
  3. Theory application: a reliable theory explains observed phenomena and predicts unobserved phenomena.
  4. Inter-theoretic relations: theories are related either across disciplines at the same time or across time within one discipline.

Three Forms of Inference

Each early stage of scientific inquiry is associated with a different logical tool:

  • Abduction formulates a hypothesis: phenomenon $e$ is observed; $H$ is judged the best explanation of $e$; therefore, $H$ is proposed.
  • Induction confirms a hypothesis: if $H$, then $e$; $e$ occurs; therefore, probably $H$.
  • Deduction refutes a hypothesis: if $H$, then $e$; not-$e$; therefore, not-$H$.

Confirmation is inductive rather than deductively conclusive. If $H$ says that all members of a class $F$ are $G$, while $e$ says that all examined members of $F$ are $G$, moving from examined cases to all cases is an inductive generalization. Confirmation therefore supports a theory only probabilistically.

Key Insight: Confirmation and refutation have different logical strengths. An observed implication can make a hypothesis more credible without proving it, whereas the failure of a required implication supports the deductive conclusion that the hypothesis is false.

The Deductive-Nomological Model

Carl Hempel connected scientific explanation to prediction. An explanation of phenomenon $E$ is scientific if the same reasoning could have predicted $E$ before it occurred. The general structure is:

  1. A general law $L$.
  2. An initial condition $I$.
  3. Therefore, phenomenon $E$.

This is the deductive-nomological model, or D-N model. It is deductive because $E$ follows necessarily from the premises, and nomological because at least one premise is a general law. If $E$ has already been observed, the argument explains it. If $E$ has not yet been observed, the same argument predicts it.

A valid D-N explanation requires more than any deductive argument. Its conclusion must be the phenomenon at issue, and its premises must include a law of nature and an initial condition. Definitions cannot substitute for laws or initial conditions, and an imperative such as an instruction to take an umbrella is not an initial condition or a logical proposition.

Key Insight: In Hempel's model, explanation and prediction differ in temporal position rather than logical form. The same law-and-condition argument is a prediction before the phenomenon is observed and an explanation afterward.

Synchronous and Diachronic Relations

A synchronous relationship connects theories from different scientific disciplines at the same time. A diachronic relationship connects an old and a new theory within the same discipline. Logical positivism interprets one theory as explaining another when the explained theory can be logically derived from the explaining theory.

Diachronic reduction is illustrated by Einstein's relative-velocity formula:

$$V = \frac{v_1+v_2}{1+\frac{v_1v_2}{c^2}}.$$

When $v_1$ and $v_2$ are very small relative to $c$, this yields the Newtonian approximation $V \approx v_1+v_2$. The newer theory therefore explains both the domain in which Newton's theory succeeded and the domain in which it failed.

When a theory is directly refuted, a new theory may be abductively proposed without a derivational relationship to the old one. When two theories compete, a decisive experiment seeks different implications: if $H_1$ predicts $e_1$ and $H_2$ predicts $e_2$, observing $e_1$ and not-$e_2$ deductively refutes $H_2$ while only inductively supporting $H_1$.

Nagel's Reduction and Bridge Laws

Synchronous reduction faces a vocabulary problem: theories in different disciplines use different terms. Ernst Nagel proposed a translation scheme using bridge laws. These statements translate terms in the theory being explained into terms belonging to the explaining theory, after which deduction becomes possible.

For example, increased temperature is translated as increased kinetic motion of molecules, while increased gas pressure is translated as an increased rate and intensity of molecular collisions with container walls. Statistical mechanics plus these bridge laws can then yield the gas law relating increased temperature to increased pressure.

Other bridge-law examples include identifying heat with kinetic molecular motion, lightning with discharge of electric charge, water with $H_2O$, and the gene with DNA. Such translations support the unity of science ideal: higher-level theories are explained through more basic theories. Diachronic derivation similarly supports the cumulative science ideal, in which a better theory explains an older theory as a limited case.

Key Insight: Bridge laws are not optional decorations. When two theories use different vocabularies, translation is the decisive step that makes deductive reduction possible.

The Copernican Revolution as Theory Change

The development from geocentric to heliocentric astronomy illustrates repeated model revision. The intuitive two-sphere model contained Earth and a sphere of stars moving from east to west. The slow eastward motion of the Sun, Moon, and five other apparent stars motivated a third sphere for the planets. Their different rates then motivated a separate sphere for each planet in a seven-sphere model.

Retrograde motion required combinations of axes, while the observation that planets glow more strongly during recession helped motivate Ptolemy's mathematical epicycle model, which departed from Aristotle's realistic sphere model. Further corrections included the eccentric for the unequal lengths of winter and summer and the equant for changes in planetary speed.

Ptolemy's model nevertheless remained inaccurate and required continuing correction. It also failed to explain why Venus stays near the Sun and why retrograde motion occurs when a planet is far from the Sun. The Copernican model explained these patterns as well as planetary brightness. Its importance therefore involved explanatory integration, not merely another isolated adjustment.

Key Insight: Adding devices can preserve a model's descriptive fit without explaining why several patterns occur together. The Copernican alternative was powerful because one framework connected brightness, the position of Venus, and retrograde motion.

Scope and Limitation of the Positivist Program

The positivist method is attractive because it is simple, clear, sensible, and apparently based only on logic and observation. It resembles an algorithm: observations and theories enter specified forms of inference, producing scientific conclusions. However, serious problems were eventually identified in every component of this method, beginning with the use of induction. The framework should therefore be understood as an elegant proposed method rather than an unproblematic final account of science.
