The inference map

Same evidence, different logical moves.

Ask what the argument is trying to do: invent a theory, support a theory, or derive what must follow. The direction and strength of the conclusion identify the inference.

Abduction

Example: Eddington observed bent starlight as Einstein predicted, so relativity gained support—but was not deductively proved.

How good is the argument?

Use the right test for the right kind.

First ask whether the argument claims certainty or only probability; then evaluate its inferential support before checking whether its premises are actually true.

Deductive argument

All or nothing
ValidNo possible true-premise, false-conclusion case + True premisesThe starting claims match reality = SoundTherefore the conclusion is true

Validity tests the connection, not the facts: a valid argument may contain false premises; it becomes sound only when every premise is true.

All premises
TRUE + Conclusion
FALSE

Example: “All planets are cheese; Earth is a planet; therefore Earth is cheese” is valid but unsound—the form works, but its first premise is false.

Non-deductive argument

A matter of degree
StrongThe conclusion is more likely true than false + True premisesThe evidence really is correct = ReliableStill not a guarantee

Strength measures probability: the premises make the conclusion more likely than not, but unlike validity they do not make a false conclusion impossible.

Weak
P(c) ≤ 0.5 → Strong
P(c) > 0.5

Example: “Most ravens are black; this bird is a raven; therefore it is probably black” can be strong and reliable even if this particular raven happens to be a rare albino.

Exam trap 1A false premise does not make a deductive argument invalid; it prevents soundness.
Exam trap 2A true conclusion does not repair an invalid inference or prove the premises supported it.
Exam trap 3False premises with a false conclusion tell you nothing by themselves about validity.
Word categoriesSentences are true or false; arguments are valid, sound, strong, weak, or reliable.

A different use of “weak” and “strong”

Is the model’s filter the right size?

Here we are judging the coverage of a definition or model—not how strongly premises support a conclusion. Imagine the model as a filter deciding which cases count.

Weak / strong argument ≠ Too weak / too strong definition or model

Too weak

Too many get in

The criterion demands too little: it includes false positives—cases that satisfy the model but are not genuine examples of the concept.

D–N model acceptsLoose filter
✓Genuine: pole height and sunlight explain the shadow. ×False positive: the shadow calculates—but does not explain—the pole’s height. ×False positive: “being hexed” is irrelevant to why salt dissolves.

Too strong

Too few get in

The criterion demands too much: it creates false negatives—genuine examples that fail the model’s unnecessarily strict requirement.

D–N model acceptsStrict filter
✓Accepted: a law and conditions deductively entail the phenomenon.

Wrongly rejected: untreated syphilis can explain Rita’s paresis without guaranteeing it, and knocking over a cup can explain a stain without an explicitly stated universal law.

Too weakToo many cases accepted ↔ Too strongToo few genuine cases accepted

The same test applies to definitions of laws: “no individuals or locations” is too weak if accidental gold generalizations still pass, yet too strong if it excludes a genuine local free-fall law such as x = 0.5gt² at Earth’s surface.
