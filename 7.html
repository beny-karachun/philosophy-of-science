Induction, Refutation, and Falsifiability

Theory Summary and Key Insights

The Positivist Scientific Method

The positivist account presents science as a set of instructions grounded solely in logic and observation. Different scientific tasks use different forms of inference. Abduction may formulate a theory from observations, induction may confirm a theory from observations, and deduction may refute a theory, derive observational predictions, explain an older theory through a newer one, or connect a theory to a more basic theory through bridging statements.

This account is attractive because it appears simple, clear, sensible, and sufficiently precise to function like an algorithm. Yet serious problems arise in both hypothesis confirmation and hypothesis refutation. The important difficulties are not all of the same kind: induction appears problematic as an inference rule, whereas deductive refutation is logically valid but difficult to apply to an isolated scientific hypothesis.

Induction in Formulation and Confirmation

Induction is used primarily to confirm hypotheses and sometimes to formulate them. Inductive generalization moves from examined cases to a general claim. Because the conclusion extends beyond the observations, difficulties with induction affect both the testing and, in some cases, the creation of hypotheses.

  • Hume's problem: An attempted justification of induction argues that examined inductions have succeeded and therefore all inductions are reliable. This argument is itself inductive and assumes the reliability it is meant to establish, making it circular. One response is that deductive rules also lack further justification and are treated as axioms.
  • The Ravens Paradox: The hypothesis that all ravens are black is logically equivalent to the claim that all non-black things are non-ravens. Inductive generalization therefore appears to let observations of brown tables, green phones, and other non-black non-ravens confirm a claim about ravens. The difficulty is that logically equivalent formulations permit apparently irrelevant evidence to count as confirmation.
  • The New Riddle of Induction: A raven is defined as bleen when it was first observed before 2050 and is black, or when it was not first observed before 2050 and is green. In 2026, every examined black raven is also bleen. The same observations therefore confirm both the familiar prediction that future ravens will be black and the strange prediction that ravens first observed from 2050 onward will be green.
  • The Tacking Paradox: If evidence confirms a conjunction and also confirms anything that follows from that conjunction, an irrelevant claim can be attached to an observation. A white shoe follows from the conjunction that the shoe is white and card reading is reliable. Card-reading reliability also follows from that conjunction. The rules would therefore allow the white shoe to confirm card reading, generalizing the problem so that any observation can confirm any hypothesis.
Key Insight: Logical equivalence does not guarantee intuitive evidential relevance. The Ravens Paradox shows that evidence can support one formulation by induction and thereby support its equivalent formulation even when the observation seems unrelated to the subject of the hypothesis.

Proposed Responses to the Inductive Paradoxes

Hempel responds to the Ravens Paradox by interpreting every universal statement of the form all Fs are Gs as applying to every object: for each object, if it is F, then it is G. A non-black raven refutes the hypothesis, while a black raven or a non-black non-raven fits and therefore confirms it. On this view, apparently irrelevant objects really are relevant. Even if this answers the confirmation problem, it does not solve the formulation problem, because observations of unrelated non-black objects could still be used to formulate incompatible hypotheses about raven colors.

Goodman proposes that a hypothesis can be inductively confirmed only when its terms are entrenched, meaning that they have been used successfully in many past generalizations. Black is entrenched, whereas bleen, non-raven, and non-black are not. This blocks the problematic inferences, but at a substantial cost: the proposal is too conservative because it prevents new scientific terms, such as electron, from being introduced.

Key Insight: A restriction can block bad inductions while also blocking scientific innovation. Goodman's appeal to entrenched terms distinguishes black from bleen through past use, but the same historical requirement makes genuinely new terminology difficult to admit.

Deductive Refutation and the Duhem-Quine Thesis

The simple positivist model of refutation uses modus tollens: if hypothesis H is correct, observation e will occur; e does not occur; therefore H is false. The deduction itself is valid. The practical problem is that a scientific hypothesis never produces an observational prediction alone. It does so only together with background assumptions, represented as H*. The actual structure is:

  1. If the combination of H and H* holds, then e follows.
  2. Observation e does not occur.
  3. Therefore, the combination of H and H* is false.
  4. Therefore, either H is false or H* is false.

This is the Duhem-Quine thesis. A failed prediction refutes the conjunction of a focal hypothesis and its background assumptions, not the focal hypothesis by itself. Scientists must decide whether to revise the theory or revise an auxiliary assumption. Consequently, contrary evidence can always be accommodated in principle by changing the background assumptions.

Neptune and Mercury

Newton's theory failed to predict the observed orbit of Uranus. Instead of immediately rejecting Newton's theory, Le Verrier revised the background assumption that no additional celestial body was affecting Uranus. He calculated the mass and orbit of a proposed planet, named Neptune before it was directly seen, and Neptune was eventually observed. The episode shows that preserving a theory by changing a background assumption can produce a successful discovery.

Deviations in Mercury's orbit led to a proposed planet called Vulcan, but repeated attempts failed to observe it. Einstein's theory of relativity eventually explained Mercury's motion. In this case, scientists treated Newton's theory rather than the relevant background assumptions as refuted. The contrast with Neptune does not defeat Duhem-Quine; it illustrates the thesis because the same logical form leaves open which component should be revised.

Key Insight: The Neptune and Mercury cases had the same logical underdetermination but different scientific outcomes. Failed prediction alone did not dictate whether Newton's theory or a background assumption should be abandoned.

Inductive Confirmation Compared with Deductive Refutation

The two problems must be distinguished carefully. For confirmation by induction, the problem lies in the inference itself because inductive generalization produces paradoxical or absurd results; applying the rule is not identified as the difficulty. For refutation by deduction, there is no defect in modus tollens. The problem lies in application: a scientific hypothesis cannot be isolated from the background assumptions required to derive its prediction.

If a hypothesis could be tested in complete isolation, deductive refutation would work. In actual scientific practice, however, observation can refute only the combined system. Thus, the positivist method of definitively falsifying an individual hypothesis cannot be applied as its simple formulation requires.

Popper's Demarcation Principle

Karl Popper sought a criterion separating scientific theories from pseudo-scientific theories. He contrasted Einstein's general theory of relativity with Marxism, Freudian psychoanalysis, and Adlerian psychology. He argued that the latter theories could interpret whatever happened as confirmation and did not specify observations they would accept as refutations.

Popper's criterion is falsifiability: a theory is scientific if it states which possible observations would refute it through deductive reasoning. Einstein's general theory of relativity, tested through the Eddington observation concerning the deflection of starlight by the Sun's gravity, served as his model of a falsifiable theory.

The Duhem-Quine thesis creates a severe problem for this criterion. If every apparent refutation can in principle be answered by changing background assumptions, no observation can definitively refute a theory. If Popper requires definitive observational falsifiability for scientific status, the combined argument implies that no theory satisfies his criterion and therefore that no theory is scientific.

Key Insight: Duhem-Quine challenges not the validity of deduction but Popper's use of definitive refutation as a boundary of science. If observations confront whole systems rather than isolated theories, falsifiability cannot straightforwardly classify individual theories.
