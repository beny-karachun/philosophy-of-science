Kuhn, Paradigms, and Scientific Method Review

Theory Summary and Key Insights

1. The Positivist Baseline

The positivist picture treats scientific knowledge as a structured movement between observations and theories. Hypotheses are formulated by abduction from theory-independent observations, confirmed by induction from such observations, and refuted by deduction when a predicted observation conflicts with what is observed. In the other direction, theories explain and predict observations by deductive reasoning from general laws.

The same logical ideal is applied to relations between theories. Theory T1 explains theory T2 when T2 logically follows from T1. This can be synchronic, between theories in different sciences, or diachronic, between a newer and an older theory. The example is Newtonian gravitation: from $a = GM/R^2$, the acceleration of a freely falling body near Earth can be derived as approximately 9.8 m/s2, so Newton's theory explains Galileo's law.

Key Insight: In the positivist model, explanation is not merely compatibility or historical succession. The decisive relation is logical derivability: the statement being explained must follow from the explaining theory together with the relevant conditions.

This supports the cumulative science ideal. A new theory should explain why the old theory succeeded where it did, explain why it failed where it did, and add new explanatory power. Science is therefore pictured as a universal project that builds progressively toward truth.

When observation contradicts a theory, three responses are possible: revise the current theory, discard it and seek a completely different theory, or treat it as approximately correct and replace it with a broader theory that explains both its successes and failures. The third response best fits the cumulative ideal.

2. Kuhn's Descriptive Pattern of Scientific Change

Kuhn begins from the history of actual scientific practice. Scientists do not normally abandon a theory as soon as an anomalous observation appears. They continue working within it, refining it and solving problems. A theory is usually replaced only when a replacement theory is available, so scientific practice does not leave a theoretical vacuum.

Even a new theory may begin with observations that it cannot accommodate, yet the scientific community may still adopt it. This loss of some previously handled observations is called Kuhn loss. Major theoretical changes are also rare rather than routine.

The recurring pattern is a long period of normal science followed by a shorter scientific revolution. Normal science is stable, conservative work within a shared framework, despite unresolved anomalies. During a revolution, the established theory is replaced by a radically different one. A new period of normal science then begins.

3. Description and Normativity

The claim that science has not historically followed the positivist recipe is descriptive. By itself, it does not refute a normative method that says how science should be conducted. It could simply mean that scientists have often failed to follow the correct method.

Kuhn therefore asks a stronger question: is the historical pattern also the best and unavoidable way for science to operate? His answer is yes. Normal science is necessary to exhaust the potential of a theory through sustained investigation. Revolutions are necessary so that inquiry does not remain trapped in an exhausted framework and can move to a theory that addresses unresolved problems while opening new research possibilities.

Key Insight: Kuhn's challenge is not merely that scientists behave differently from the positivist ideal. He argues that the alternation between conservative normal science and disruptive revolutions is both productive and, because theories are embedded in paradigms, unavoidable in principle.

4. Paradigms as Worldviews

A theory is embedded in a broader paradigm, a worldview or set of conceptual glasses through which scientists perceive and organize reality. A paradigm contains four interconnected components:

  • Perception: the categories and conceptual framework through which observations are interpreted.
  • Semantics: the meanings of the terms used within the paradigm.
  • Methodology: the rules for conducting research and evaluating theories.
  • Ontology: assumptions about what exists and what the world's basic constituents are.

Because a paradigm is broader than a theory, replacing a paradigm changes more than a set of equations. It changes what scientists see as relevant evidence, what their terms mean, which methods count as legitimate, and what kinds of entities they take to exist.

5. Theory and Observation

Kuhn argues that observations are theory-laden. Scientists always observe through conceptual categories supplied by a paradigm, so there are no completely neutral, theory-independent facts available to all paradigms in exactly the same form.

This challenges every positivist relation between observation and theory. Neutral observations were supposed to guide hypothesis formulation, confirmation, and refutation, and they were also supposed to be the independent targets of explanation and prediction. Without neutral observations, these processes become internal interactions between a paradigm and observations already shaped by it. Kuhn describes normal scientific work as puzzle-solving: the theory becomes better at solving problems defined within its own paradigm, but this does not establish a paradigm-independent approach to truth.

6. Relations Between Theories

The semantic component of paradigms leads to holism in meaning: a term receives its meaning from its place in the entire network of concepts. The same word can therefore have different meanings in different paradigms. The term mass in Newtonian theory and the term mass in Einsteinian theory serve as the central example.

If the terms of two theories do not retain the same meanings, one theory cannot simply be translated into and logically derived from the other. Bridge laws do not solve the problem because any bridge law is itself formulated within some paradigm. Kuhn therefore says that scientists in different paradigms operate in different worlds, and paradigm change resembles conversion rather than a step-by-step deduction.

Key Insight: The obstacle to inter-theoretic explanation is not only missing evidence. If meanings, observations, and standards are paradigm-dependent, then the logical comparison demanded by positivism lacks a neutral language, neutral facts, and neutral evaluation criteria.

7. Theory Choice and the Cumulative Ideal

Methodological standards belong to paradigms, so there is no external criterion by which competing paradigms can be decisively compared. A crucial experiment cannot be fully neutral because both the observations and the rule for what counts as decisive are paradigm-dependent.

The transition between paradigms therefore occurs in leaps rather than as a linear accumulation. In Kuhn's strongest formulation, one paradigm does not logically explain its predecessor, theories cannot be compared from a neutral standpoint, and replacement is a change rather than demonstrable progress toward an independent truth.

8. Additional Scientific-Reasoning Distinctions

  • Popper and falsifiability: a theory that can explain opposite outcomes is not thereby strong. Its flexibility may make it impossible to refute and therefore mark it as pseudoscientific, even though supporters can collect many apparent confirmations.
  • Induction: arguing that induction will work because it worked in the past uses induction to justify induction and is circular. The positivist confirmation method can also produce absurd confirmations through unusual but logically permitted descriptions.
  • Hume: the metaphysical argument associated with Hume's fork treats natural laws as contingent rather than logically necessary.
  • Explanation: defining scientific explanation by appeal to causes does not automatically clarify the issue, because the concept of cause may be no clearer than the concept of explanation. The deductive-nomological model also permits both the height-to-shadow and shadow-to-height derivations, revealing a symmetry problem.
  • Duhem-Quine: deduction itself is not defective, but applying deduction to refute one scientific hypothesis is difficult because tests involve a wider set of assumptions.
  • Laws and reduction: the Armstrong-Dretske-Tully view distinguishes a law from an accidental generalization by a connection between properties. Multiple realizability allows mental types to fail to match single brain-state types even when each mental instance has a physical instance. Reduction between sciences still requires bridge laws; shared teleological features do not remove that requirement.

A scientific theory may be true or false, but the review framework denies that its truth can be known with certainty. Deductive validity is also distinct from the actual truth values of premises and conclusion: finding a case with false premises and a false conclusion does not by itself prove invalidity. For strong nondeductive arguments, true premises and a true conclusion support calling the argument reliable rather than deductively sound.
