Scientific Method and Logical Positivism

Theory Summary and Key Insights

What Is Being Criticized?

Criticism of science can concern its authority, but a more specific target is the scientific method: the way an ideal science ought to be conducted. Critiques may arise from within the scientific community or from external religious and cultural perspectives. Internal critiques are especially rigorous because they identify problems in the methodological framework used by science itself.

The scientific method is normative rather than merely descriptive. It is intended to state how scientists ought to work, not simply report how they actually work. In its ideal form, it would resemble a foolproof instruction manual: exact directions that guarantee appropriate results without depending on the user's intuition, experience, or exceptional skill.

Key Insight: A defect in an ideal method is more serious than a mistake in applying that method. An application error may be avoided by a more careful practitioner, whereas an intrinsic flaw remains even when the instructions are followed exactly.

The Aims of Science and the Role of Theory

A scientific method should be judged by whether it enables science to achieve its aims. The primary aims are:

  • Providing reliable explanations of natural phenomena.
  • Producing accurate predictions of natural phenomena.
  • Supporting the development of successful technologies.

Technology depends on successful explanation and prediction, so those two tasks are foundational. Science pursues them by constructing scientific theories. A good method must therefore guide scientists both in generating reliable theories and in using those theories to explain and predict phenomena.

Ideally, this guidance would constitute an algorithm that could, in principle, be programmed into a computer. Despite centuries of scientific progress, no flawless algorithm of this kind has been devised.

The Tasks a Comprehensive Method Must Address

  1. Theory generation: How should scientific theories be produced?
  2. Theory evaluation: How should theories be confirmed or falsified?
  3. Theory application: How should theories explain observed phenomena and predict phenomena?
  4. Inter-theoretic relations: How should competing theories be compared, and how should theories relate across time and disciplines?
  5. Science and values: Whether values play a role in science and, if so, what that role should be.

Relations between new and older theories in the same discipline are diachronic. Relations between theories belonging to different scientific disciplines are synchronic.

Two Categories of Methodological Failure

Proposed scientific methods have exhibited two opposite kinds of defect. First, a method may permit results that it should exclude, such as absurd hypotheses, unsupported predictions, irrelevant confirmations, or backwards explanations. Examples include predicting that the next raven will be yellow, treating a white shoe as confirmation that all ravens are black, or using today's constellation to explain yesterday's constellation.

Second, a method may fail to permit operations that science requires, such as refuting a theory through a conflicting observation, choosing between competing theories, or explaining sciences such as chemistry, biology, and psychology in terms of physics.

Key Insight: Scientific success does not prove that the formal guidebook is flawless. Scientists avoid many outcomes permitted by the guidebook because they supplement it with intuition and common sense, resources that have not yet been successfully converted into an exact algorithm.

The Vienna Circle and the Need for Explicit Formulation

The Vienna Circle, active from 1924 to 1936, included natural scientists, social scientists, mathematicians, and philosophers. Its members sought to formulate explicitly the method practiced since the seventeenth-century Scientific Revolution and exemplified by Isaac Newton. Explicit formulation was necessary because even successful practitioners could misunderstand or misdescribe their own methods.

Newton described hypothesis formation as inductive generalization, in which general laws are derived from particular observations. Repeated observations of black ravens can suggest the general hypothesis that all ravens are black. However, merely looking at observations does not plausibly generate a sophisticated law such as Newton's universal law of gravitation, $F = GMM'/R^2$. Formulating such a law requires more than mechanically generalizing the vocabulary already present in observations.

Key Insight: A procedure that can generalize a visible pattern need not be capable of creating a theoretically novel law. Newton's own description therefore illustrates the difference between successfully practicing science and accurately specifying the method responsible for that success.

Rationalism and Empiricism

Newtonian science is distinguished from much pre-Scientific Revolution science by its reliance on empirical observation. Rationalism and empiricism differ over the ultimate criterion for evaluating scientific propositions and theories.

  • Rationalism gives priority to pure reason. A theory consistent with the principles of reason is treated as true, and reason should prevail when it conflicts with sensory observation because the senses are regarded as fallible.
  • Empiricism gives priority to sensory observation. Observation should prevail in a conflict because reasoning can mislead us and empirical evidence is treated as the more trustworthy foundation.

Logical Empiricism or Logical Positivism

Logical empiricism adds a crucial analysis to classical empiricism. A scientific theory is a set of propositions, while observed facts are expressed as empirical propositions. The relation between theory and world can therefore be treated as a relation between theoretical and empirical propositions, and relations among propositions are governed by logic.

Knowledge about the world may consequently arise through direct observation and through applying logical rules to empirical propositions. Logical empiricism, also called logical positivism, requires scientific operations to rely only on empirical observations and logic. Its method seeks to explain how theories are generated from observations, evaluated by observation and logic, applied to explanation and prediction, and related to other theories.

Key Insight: Logical positivism does not replace observation with logic. It combines empirical propositions with trusted logical relations, thereby attempting to turn the connection between evidence, theory, explanation, and prediction into a systematic method.

Achievement and Foundational Difficulty

The Vienna Circle produced systematic guidance for theory generation, confirmation, refutation, explanation, prediction, relations between theories, and the relationship between science and values. The resulting framework was appealing, intuitive, and influential.

Beginning in the 1960s, however, fundamental problems accumulated. If the positivist guidebook were executed mechanically, it could produce absurd and unjustifiable theories, predictions, and explanations as well as reasonable ones. Radical revision or replacement therefore appears necessary, yet no satisfactory alternative has been found. Scientists continue to work largely within the positivist framework while relying on judgment to compensate for its documented weaknesses.
