Laws of Nature and Scientific Explanation

Theory Summary and Key Insights

Why Laws of Nature Matter

Reliable theories are needed to perform two central scientific tasks: prediction and explanation. A standard predictive argument combines a general law with an initial condition and deductively derives a phenomenon. For example, the law that freezing water expands, together with the condition that a lake will freeze, supports the prediction that the lake's volume will increase.

A merely true pattern is not automatically a law of nature. A team might always have won whenever a particular spectator attended, yet that accidental regularity does not justify predicting another victory. The philosophical challenge is therefore to distinguish laws of nature from accidental generalizations.

Humean and Anti-Humean Accounts of Laws

Defining a law as any true generalization is too weak because it admits accidental regularities. A first Humean refinement excludes statements limited to particular individuals or locations. This is still too weak because a statement such as every existing gold sample weighing less than ten tons can be accidentally true. It is also too strong because genuine laws may concern a single universe or apply only under local conditions, as with $x = 0.5gt^2$ at Earth's surface.

A second Humean proposal requires a law to be formulated entirely in general terms. This remains too weak because singular descriptions can be replaced artificially with general descriptions without removing their accidental character. The strongest Humean proposal identified is the Ramsey-Lewis position, but its details are not developed here.

Anti-Humeans instead propose that a law is a true generalization that is also necessary. Hume challenges this proposal in two ways:

  • Epistemic argument: empirical experience reveals constant conjunction between types of events, not necessity itself. Strict epistemic empiricism therefore provides no justification for belief in necessity.
  • Metaphysical argument: Hume's fork classifies statements as either necessary and a priori or non-necessary and a posteriori. Since scientific laws are known a posteriori, the argument concludes that they are not necessary.

The referential theory of language challenges Hume's fork by allowing necessary a posteriori identities. Examples include the identity between Madonna and Louise Ciccone, and the scientific identities water and $H_2O$, lightning and electrical-energy discharge, and heat and average molecular kinetic energy. These cases directly challenge the claim that every a posteriori scientific statement must be non-necessary.

The epistemic argument can also be challenged by an argument from unacceptable consequences. Science relies on entities such as electrons, forces, and fields even though they are not directly experienced. If strict epistemic empiricism ruled out belief in all such entities, much of scientific theory would have to be abandoned. Rejecting that strict premise is therefore presented as less costly.

Key Insight: A definition can fail in two independent directions. It is too weak if it includes cases that are not instances of the concept, and too strong if it excludes genuine instances. One definition can have both defects at once.

The Armstrong-Dretske-Tully View

The prevailing constructive anti-Humean account presented is the Armstrong-Dretske-Tully view. Objects possess properties, and fixed relationships hold between properties. A law is necessary because of the relationship between the properties it describes. Thus, the expansion of freezing water follows from a fixed connection involving the relevant properties. By contrast, even a true association between Galileo wearing white and cloudy weather would not be a law because no corresponding connection exists between those properties.

The dispute between Humean and anti-Humean accounts remains unresolved. Consequently, no accepted criterion is available here for deciding in every case whether a prediction or explanation is scientific by virtue of being based on a genuine law.

The Deductive-Nomological Model

Logical Empiricism proposes that explanation and prediction have the same deductive structure:

  1. A general law $L$.
  2. An initial condition $I$.
  3. A phenomenon $e$ that follows deductively from $L$ and $I$.

If $e$ has already been observed, the argument is called an explanation. If $e$ has not yet been observed, it is called a prediction. As a model of explanation, this is the Deductive-Nomological model, or D-N model.

Because the D-N model defines scientific explanation, it should apply only to explanations and to all explanations. Counterexamples show that it satisfies neither requirement.

Why the D-N Model Is Too Weak

The symmetry problem arises because deduction can proceed in a direction opposite to explanation. Geometry, a shadow's length, and the Sun's angle can be used to calculate a flagpole's height. Yet the shadow does not explain why the pole has that height; rather, the pole's height helps explain its shadow. Similarly, Newton's laws and Wednesday's stellar configuration might permit calculation of Tuesday's configuration, but Wednesday's configuration does not explain Tuesday's.

An explanation of how a value was calculated must not be confused with an explanation of the phenomenon itself. Evidence or data may reveal a phenomenon without causing or explaining it.

The relevance problem supplies another counterexample. If all salt dissolves in water, then all hexed salt dissolves in water. The D-N structure could therefore cite being hexed as an initial condition in a valid deduction, although the hex is irrelevant to the dissolution. Deductive validity alone does not ensure explanatory relevance.

Key Insight: Logical derivability is not explanatory direction. A phenomenon can provide enough information to infer its cause, while still failing to explain that cause.

Why the D-N Model Is Too Strong

The model also excludes acceptable causal explanations that do not deductively entail their outcomes. Untreated syphilis may cause paresis without producing it in every case. Rita's untreated syphilis can therefore explain her paresis even though the premise does not deductively imply that she would develop it. Likewise, knocking over a coffee cup can explain a carpet stain without an explicitly stated universal law.

The objection that a hidden law must underlie such explanations does not remove the problem. The explanations appear acceptable even when no law is identified, and they would remain acceptable even if an underlying law existed but could not be stated explicitly.

Logical and Causal Arrows

The criticisms reveal a fundamental difference between prediction and explanation:

  • Prediction follows the logical arrow: $H$ predicts $e$ when $H$ logically entails $e$.
  • Explanation follows the causal arrow: $H$ explains $e$ when $H$ is the cause of $e$.

The arrows need not point in the same direction. Earlier stellar conditions may both predict and explain later ones. Later stellar conditions may permit a prediction or reconstruction of earlier ones without explaining them. Untreated syphilis may explain paresis even when it cannot predict it deductively.

Key Insight: Prediction and explanation can separate in both directions: a premise may predict without explaining, and it may explain without predicting. The temporal status of the conclusion as observed or unobserved is therefore not the only difference.

Overall Assessment and the Causal Alternative

The D-N model is too weak because it licenses non-explanatory deductions, and too strong because it excludes genuine non-deductive explanations. Either defect alone is enough to reject it as a satisfactory model of scientific explanation. Its deductive structure makes it a model of scientific prediction, not a successful model of scientific explanation.

The causal powers view proposes that $H$ explains $e$ precisely when $H$ causes $e$. This captures explanatory direction, but it creates another difficulty: there is no widely accepted definition of causality. Hume's empiricism intensifies the problem because causal relations themselves are not observed; only constant conjunction is observed. If belief in causality is unjustified, a causal model of explanation cannot be sustained.

Key Insight: Replacing deduction with causation identifies what the D-N model lacks, but it does not complete the theory. The proposed solution inherits the unresolved problem of defining and justifying causality itself.

Other models of scientific explanation have also faced substantial criticism. The resulting conclusion is that there is no presently accepted scientific method for providing explanations.
