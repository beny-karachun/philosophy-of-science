Logical Positivism and the Scientific Method

Theory Summary and Key Insights

The Purpose and Scope of Scientific Method

The scientific method is a normative guide to how science ought to be conducted: it functions as an instruction manual for scientific practice. A good method helps science achieve its fundamental goals of providing reliable explanations of natural phenomena, enabling reliable predictions, and supporting the development of successful technologies. Science pursues these goals by formulating reliable theories and using them appropriately.

A complete scientific method must provide guidance for several connected tasks:

  • Theory formation: generating scientific theories.
  • Theory evaluation: confirming or refuting theories through observation.
  • Theory application: using theories to predict and explain phenomena.
  • Inter-theoretic relations: choosing between competitors and relating theories across fields and historical periods.
  • Science and values: analyzing the connection between scientific inquiry and values.

Logical Positivism

Logical positivism, also called logical empiricism, was formulated by the Vienna Circle, a group of scientists and philosophers in early twentieth-century Vienna. Inspired by the success of Newtonian physics, they sought to articulate the method that Newton had used. Logical positivism recognizes only two reliable sources of knowledge: empirical observation and logic. Legitimate scientific knowledge must therefore consist of direct observations or claims logically derived from observations.

The positivist project applies these two tools to every major scientific task. It offers systematic and intuitive instructions for generating, testing, applying, and relating theories. Although foundational problems began to accumulate from the 1960s onward, no satisfactory replacement has been established, so scientific practice continues to be strongly shaped by the positivist framework.

Key Insight: The central ambition of logical positivism is not merely to value evidence. It is to reconstruct every scientific task using only observation and logic, without relying on any additional source of knowledge.

Theory Formation: From Observation to a Candidate Theory

Positivist theory formation begins with an observation that needs to be explained. The scientist searches for a theory, understood as a set of laws, from which that observation can be logically deduced. If the theory were true, the observed result would have to follow.

For example, suppose a resistor of resistance $R=5$ produces current $I=0.2$ at voltage $V=1$ and current $I=0.4$ at voltage $V=2$. Both of the following theories fit those observations:

$$I=\frac{V}{R}$$

$$I=\frac{V^2-2V+2}{R}$$

The observed cases therefore do not uniquely determine a theory. Multiple laws can logically entail the same initial evidence, so further observations are needed to distinguish the alternatives.

Key Insight: Explaining the observations used to construct a theory is not enough to establish that theory. Distinct theories can fit the same known data while implying sharply different results in untested cases.

Theory Evaluation: Confirmation and Refutation

A candidate theory must undergo observational testing. Scientists deduce additional observations that the theory logically entails and then check whether those observations occur. If an entailed observation occurs, the theory is confirmed, meaning supported rather than proved. If the entailed observation does not occur, the theory is refuted, and inquiry returns to the theory-formation stage to seek an alternative.

The two resistor theories illustrate the value of a discriminating test. With $V=10$ and $R=5$, the first theory predicts:

$$I=\frac{10}{5}=2$$

The second predicts:

$$I=\frac{10^2-2(10)+2}{5}=16.4$$

An observation under these conditions can therefore distinguish the theories, unlike the original measurements at $V=1$ and $V=2$.

The Semmelweis Case

Carl Hempel used the work of Ignaz Semmelweis to illustrate positivist theory formation and evaluation. Between 1844 and 1848, 260 mothers died in one ward of a Vienna hospital. Several proposed explanations failed observational tests:

  • Cosmic-terrestrial atmospheric changes were undermined by comparison with other hospitals, where the expected difference was absent.
  • Overcrowding was undermined because the second ward was more crowded.
  • Injuries caused by inexperienced students were undermined by the greater seriousness of natural childbirth injuries and by the unchanged mortality rate after the number of students was halved.
  • The birthing position hypothesis was undermined when changing from birth on the back to birth on the side did not change mortality.

A decisive clue arose when a person accidentally cut during an autopsy died with symptoms like those of the mothers. Semmelweis proposed that doctors transferred material from corpses into the bloodstreams of birthing women. Requiring handwashing with calcium hypochlorite after dissections and before contact with patients dramatically reduced mortality, confirming the hypothesis. A broader hypothesis concerning decaying matter from living organisms was subsequently supported when handwashing after any such contact reduced mortality further.

Prediction, Risk, and Retrodiction

An observation logically necessitated by a theory is one of that theory's predictions. Testing therefore consists of deducing predictions and checking whether they occur. A particularly meaningful test uses a risky prediction: an outcome unlikely to occur unless the theory is true. Eddington's 1919 test of the general-relativistic prediction that light rays bend near heavy objects exemplifies this strategy.

After a theory passes risky tests, it is treated as reliable enough to predict other phenomena. General relativity, for example, predicts that clocks on satellites at an altitude of about 20,000 kilometers run faster because gravitational force there is about four times weaker. Satellite navigation must correct the resulting cumulative errors in ground-distance measurements.

Prediction is not limited to future events. A theory can also infer a past event; this is called retrodiction. Observational findings support the retrodiction that approximately 65 million years ago a giant asteroid struck the Yucatán Peninsula and contributed to an extinction involving about 70 percent of animal species then living, including the dinosaurs.

Explanation and the Prediction Standard

Predictions concern what is not yet observed and are challenging because they involve risk, although they cannot always be checked immediately. Explanations concern what is already known and can be checked immediately, but hindsight makes weak explanations easy to invent. Hempel therefore proposed holding explanations to the same standard as predictions:

A hypothesis $H$ scientifically explains an observation $E$ if $H$ would have allowed $E$ to be predicted before $E$ occurred.

For example, at a constant voltage of 10 volts, replacing a 5-ohm resistor with a 10-ohm resistor reduces current from 2 amperes to 1 ampere. The law $I=V/R$ explains this result because it would have predicted the decrease in advance.

Key Insight: In Hempel's account, prediction and explanation share the same logical relation: the theory entails the observation. Their principal difference is temporal or epistemic—the predicted result has not yet been observed, whereas the explained result is already known.

Inter-Theoretic Relations

Synchronic relations connect theories in different scientific fields at the same time. Logical positivism ideally arranges the sciences hierarchically, beginning with theories about the smallest objects, such as particle physics, and proceeding through chemistry, biology, psychology, and sociology. Each lower-level theory should explain the theory above it. The axioms of a higher-level science would thereby become theorems derivable from a lower-level science. This unified hierarchy is the Unity of Science Ideal.

Diachronic relations connect theories within the same science at different times. An old theory can be replaced in two ways:

  1. Discarding: a refuted theory is completely replaced, as geocentrism was replaced by heliocentrism.
  2. Explanation or reduction: the older theory becomes a special case of a broader and more general theory. Einstein's physics explains Newton's physics, which explains Galileo's dynamics and Kepler's astronomy.

The resulting picture of progress from theory to theory is the Cumulative Science Ideal. The synchronic ideal unifies different disciplines at one time, whereas the diachronic ideal presents historical scientific progress as either replacement or reduction.

Key Insight: The Unity of Science Ideal and the Cumulative Science Ideal organize theories along different dimensions. The first seeks vertical integration across disciplines at the same time; the second seeks continuity or justified replacement within a discipline over time.

The Foundational Difficulty

The positivist method remains influential despite fundamental flaws. It can permit outcomes that a sound scientific method should exclude, such as arbitrary hypotheses, implausible predictions, irrelevant confirmations, or backward explanations. At the same time, it can fail to license activities that science should support, such as straightforward refutation by a contradictory observation, deciding between competing theories, or reducing every higher-level science to physics.

Scientists nevertheless succeed partly because they use guiding intuitions beyond the official method. The difficulty is that these intuitions have not been translated into clear and unambiguous instructions. The methodological challenge is therefore not simply to identify flaws, but to formulate a more adequate replacement without losing the systematic guidance that made logical positivism attractive.
