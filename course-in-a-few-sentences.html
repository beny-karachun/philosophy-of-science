01The positivist blueprint

Observation and logic are supposed to run science.

Logical positivism says that scientific knowledge should be based only on observation and logic. A deductive argument is valid when no possible case has true premises and a false conclusion, and it is sound when it is valid and its premises are actually true; a non-deductive argument is strong when its premises make the conclusion more likely than not, and reliable when it is strong and has true premises. Within this framework, scientists use abduction to formulate a theory—as when Semmelweis proposed that doctors carried decaying material because it best explained maternal deaths—induction to confirm a theory without proving it—as Eddington’s observation of bent starlight supported Einstein—and deduction to predict, explain, or refute by modus tollens.

02Testing attacked

Every step from evidence to theory develops a problem.

Popper argues that Adler’s ability to explain both saving a drowning child and ignoring the child demonstrates unfalsifiability, not theoretical strength. Hume shows that saying “induction worked before, so it will work again” circularly uses induction to justify itself; the Raven Paradox lets a white shoe confirm “all ravens are black” because it is a non-black non-raven; and Goodman shows that artificial descriptions can make the same present evidence support incompatible future projections—as in exam examples connecting a green sock today with a blue horse after 2050. Finally, Duhem–Quine shows that a failed prediction refutes the conjunction of a theory and its background assumptions, not the theory alone: scientists preserved Newton by proposing Neptune when Uranus behaved anomalously, but eventually changed the central theory when relativity explained Mercury.

03Explanation and laws

Logical prediction is not automatically genuine explanation.

The deductive-nomological model derives an observation from a law and initial conditions, as Newton’s a = GM/R² explains Galileo’s measured acceleration of approximately 9.8 m/s². As a model of explanation, however, it is too weak because it admits false positives: a shadow can be used to calculate a flagpole’s height without explaining that height, a later stellar configuration can reconstruct an earlier one without causing it, and “being hexed” can appear in a valid but irrelevant salt-dissolving argument. It is also too strong because it excludes genuine explanations that do not guarantee their outcomes: untreated syphilis can explain paresis without every untreated patient developing it, and knocking over a cup can explain a stain without an explicitly stated universal law. Appealing to causes restores direction, but “cause” may be no clearer than “explanation.”

!Keep the labels straight: too weak means the model includes non-explanations; too strong means it excludes genuine explanations.
04Natural laws

A true pattern is not necessarily a law.

Prediction needs genuine laws rather than accidental truths: “whenever Lucy wears green, the team wins” could be true while lacking any real connection between wearing green and winning. The Armstrong–Dretske–Tully account therefore describes laws as necessary relations between properties, such as the relevant properties of water producing expansion when water freezes. This distinguishes law-like connection from mere repeated coincidence, although calling the relation “nomological necessity” leaves philosophers asking what that necessity actually is.

05Unity of science

The sciences should connect—but the mind resists simple reduction.

The positivist ideal seeks synchronous unity by deriving chemistry from physics, biology from chemistry, and psychology from biology through bridge laws that translate between their vocabularies. The project becomes difficult because multiple realizability allows one mental type, such as pain, to be realized by different physical systems; Searle argues that physical input-output descriptions may omit genuine understanding or consciousness; and Jackson’s Mary knows every physical fact about color while living in a black-and-white room yet appears to learn something new when she first experiences red.

06Theory change and Kuhn

Scientific change may replace the whole rulebook.

The cumulative ideal seeks diachronic progress through decisive experiments and by deriving old theories as restricted cases of new ones, as Einsteinian velocity yields Newtonian velocity at low speeds. Kuhn, however, argues that a paradigm—the community’s shared scientific rulebook and worldview—shapes perception, meanings, ontology, and methodology: Newtonian and Einsteinian “mass” may not mean exactly the same thing, scientists in different paradigms may interpret observations differently, and their standards cannot automatically act as neutral judges. A revolution may therefore involve Kuhn loss, with some old successes initially abandoned rather than perfectly preserved.

07The final position

Use the method, but do not mistake it for an infallible algorithm.

Despite these criticisms, science remains our best tool for explaining reality, predicting events, and producing technology. We therefore continue using the positivist method while remembering that observations may be theory-laden, confirmation never proves a theory, failed predictions implicate background assumptions, valid deductions need not be explanations, reductions may lose meaning or experience, and comparisons between paradigms may lack neutral standards. The course’s final lesson is not to abandon science, but to practice it with philosophical awareness of what each logical move can—and cannot—establish.
